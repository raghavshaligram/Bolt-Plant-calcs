<h2 id="what-is-companion-planting">What Is Companion Planting?</h2>
<p>
  Companion planting is the practice of growing certain plants near each other because of a real, practical benefit one gives the other &mdash; better pest control, improved growth, or more efficient use of garden space. It's not a single mechanism but a handful of them working in combination. Some pairings work through <strong>scent-based pest deterrence</strong>: aromatic herbs like dill and chives mask the smell of a vegetable that pests would otherwise home in on, or attract the predatory insects that eat those pests. Others work through <strong>nutrient sharing</strong> &mdash; beans and other legumes pull nitrogen from the air and fix it in the soil, which benefits heavy-feeding neighbors planted nearby. And some are simply about <strong>physical space and shade</strong>: a tall plant can shelter a shade-tolerant one beneath it, or a sprawling ground-cover crop can shade out weeds around a taller plant's base. None of this is exact science &mdash; results vary by climate, soil, and garden layout &mdash; but the pairings below reflect the relationships gardeners and extension offices see repeat reliably across regions.
</p>

<h2 id="common-vegetable-pairings">Common Vegetable Pairings</h2>
<p>
  <strong>Tomatoes and basil</strong> are the single most-repeated pairing in companion planting, and for good reason: basil's strong scent is widely credited with confusing pests that target tomatoes, and many gardeners find it improves how the tomato plant grows alongside it. Onions and chives make good tomato neighbors for a similar reason &mdash; their sharp scent works as a pest deterrent without competing heavily for space.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>Mint deserves a specific warning</strong> in any vegetable bed: it's an aggressive spreader that will crowd out neighboring plants within a season if left in open ground, regardless of what it's planted next to. If you want mint's pest-deterrent benefits near your vegetables, keep it in a buried pot with the rim above soil level, not planted directly in the bed.
</p>

<h2 id="herb-companion-planting">Herb Companion Planting</h2>
<p>
  This herb companion planting chart covers four of the most common kitchen herbs, and herbs pull double duty in most gardens &mdash; both as a harvestable crop and as a pest-deterrent companion for whatever vegetables surround them. <strong>Basil</strong> pairs well with tomatoes and peppers specifically. <strong>Dill</strong> is genuinely useful near kale and other brassicas, since it attracts the predatory wasps and other beneficial insects that prey on cabbage worms, and it's a classic companion for cucumbers for the same pest-control reason. <strong>Chives</strong>, like other alliums, work well near tomatoes and kale but should be kept away from beans specifically &mdash; alliums are well documented to stunt bean growth. <strong>Mint</strong> is the outlier: it's not incompatible with other herbs chemically, but its spreading habit makes it a poor open-ground neighbor for basil, dill, or chives, all of which it will simply out-compete for space over time.
</p>

<p>
  Pest control is where companion planting earns its keep in most home gardens. <strong>Marigolds</strong> are the best-documented example: certain varieties (French marigolds in particular) release compounds from their roots that suppress root-knot nematodes in the surrounding soil, which is why they're a common border planting around vegetable beds. <strong>Dill and chives</strong> work through a different mechanism &mdash; both attract predatory and parasitic insects (ladybugs, lacewings, parasitic wasps) that feed on aphids and caterpillars, effectively recruiting free pest control instead of repelling pests directly. Used together around a vegetable bed &mdash; marigolds along the border, dill and chives interplanted among the crops &mdash; these three do most of the pest-control work that companion planting is known for.
</p>

<h2 id="using-this-in-a-raised-bed-or-small-garden">Using This in a Raised Bed or Small Garden</h2>
<p>
  A companion planting chart tells you <em>what</em> to plant together; it doesn't tell you <em>how far apart</em> to actually put things, and in a raised bed or small garden that second question matters just as much as the first. Good pairings still need real spacing to avoid overcrowding &mdash; cramming basil in too tightly around tomato plants can create the same airflow and disease problems as any other overcrowded bed, good companion or not. </p>
